Introduction

A simple pendulum is a device made of a weight, typically a bob or mass, suspended from a fixed pivot and supported by a string, allowing the weight to swing back and forth freely under the influence of gravity.

Pendulums were the world’s most accurate timekeeping technology until the 1930s. The pendulum clock, invented by Christiaan Huygens in 1656, became the standard timekeeper for homes and offices for nearly three centuries, achieving remarkable accuracy of about one second per year before being supplanted by quartz clocks. Pendulums have also found applications in scientific instruments such as accelerometers and seismometers, and have historically been used as gravimeters to measure gravitational acceleration and even as standards of length. The term "pendulum" is derived from the Neo-Latin "pendulus," meaning "hanging."

Italian polymath Galileo Galilei pioneered investigations into the properties of pendulums, beginning his inquiries around 1602. The earliest documented interest in pendulums by Galileo dates back to approximately 1588, when he recorded observations about the behavior of oscillating objects in his posthumously published work titled "On Motion". Here, he noted that heavier objects tended to sustain their swinging motion for longer durations compared to lighter counterparts. Galileo’s biographer, Vincenzo Viviani, claimed that Galileo’s fascination with pendulums was sparked around 1582 by observing the swinging motion of a chandelier in Pisa Cathedral. Galileo’s seminal discoveries included the crucial property of isochronism, which enabled pendulums to function as reliable timekeepers, as the period of oscillation remained relatively constant regardless of the amplitude of the swing. Additionally, Galileo found that the period was independent of the mass of the pendulum bob, but proportional to the square root of the length of the pendulum. Galileo first employed free-swinging pendulums in simple timing applications. Building upon this, in 1602 Santorio Santori invented a device termed the pulsilogium, which utilized a pendulum to measure a patient’s pulse. Later, in 1641, Galileo dictated a design for a mechanism to sustain the swinging motion of a pendulum, which has been characterized as the first pendulum clock.

The experiments described below are intended to empirically evaluate and validate the theoretical model of the simple pendulum through testing and comparison with experimental observations.To do so, the relationships between the period of the simple pendulum and its initial amplitude, length, and mass of the pendulum bob have been investigated. Additionally, the gravitational acceleration at the location where the experiment was conducted has been calculated using the measured period of the pendulum, and the accuracy of this calculation has been analyzed by considering the isochronism at small amplitudes.Furthermore, the last experiment aimed to determine the damping coefficient, assuming that the damping was caused primarily by frictional forces acting continuously on the pendulum as it swung back and forth.

Theory

A simple pendulum is an idealized model consisting of a point mass suspended by a mass-less, un-stretchable string. When the point mass is pulled to one side of its straight-down equilibrium position and released, it oscillates about the equilibrium position.


The anticlockwise torque around the pivot point is given by, \[\tau = -mg\sin{\theta}\cdot L\]
Using the Newton’s second law for rotational motion, \[\begin{aligned} \tau & = I \frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} \\ \therefore -mgL\sin{\theta}\cdot & = mL^2 \frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} \\ \therefore \frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} & = -\frac{g}{L}\sin{\theta} \end{aligned}\]

Using Small Angle Approximation

Since \(\theta \approx 0\) the equation (4) can be reduced to a simpler harmonic motion equation.

\[\frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} = -\frac{g}{L}\theta\]

By comparing it with the characteristic form of a S.H.M, \[\frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} = -\omega^2\theta\]

The period (T),\[T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \label{small_angle}\]

Without Using Small Angle Approximation

Using equation (4), \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} & = -\frac{g}{L}\sin{\theta} = \frac{d \dot \theta}{dt} = \frac{d \dot \theta}{d\theta} \frac{d \theta}{dt} = \dot \theta \frac{d \dot \theta}{d\theta} \\ \therefore \int -\frac{g}{L}\sin{\theta} d \theta & = \int \dot \theta d \dot \theta \end{aligned}\] If the bob was released at an angle of \(\alpha\),

\[\int^\theta_\alpha -\frac{g}{L}\sin{u} du = \int^{\dot \theta}_0 vdv\] Hence, \[\dot \theta = \frac{d\theta}{dt} = \sqrt{\frac{2g}{L}(\cos{\theta}-\cos{\alpha})}\]
Since \(\cos{\theta} = 1 -2 \sin^2{\theta/2}\), \[\frac{d\theta}{dt} = 2\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}(\sin^2{\alpha/2}-\sin^2{\theta/2})}\\ \] Substituting \(b = \sin{\alpha/2}\) and \(\sin{\phi} = \frac{\sin{\theta/2}}{b}\) \[\frac{d\theta}{b\cos{\phi}} = 2\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}dt\]\[b\cos{\phi}\cdot d\phi = \frac{\cos{\theta/2}}{2} d\theta\] Therefore, \[\frac{d\phi}{\cos{\theta/2}} =\frac{d\phi}{\sqrt{1 - b^2\sin^2{\phi}}} = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}dt\]
Then integrating both sides, \[\int^{2\pi}_0\frac{d\phi}{{\sqrt{1 - b^2\sin^2{\phi}}}} = \int^{T}_0{}\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}dt = T \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}\]
But, \[\frac{1}{{\sqrt{1 - b^2\sin^2{\phi}}}} = 1 + \frac{b^{2} \sin^{2}{\left(\phi \right)}}{2} + \frac{3 b^{4} \sin^{4}{\left(\phi \right)}}{8} + ...\]
Hence, \[\begin{aligned} \int^{2\pi}_0\frac{d\phi}{{\sqrt{1 - b^2\sin^2{\phi}}}} & = \int^{2\pi}_0 \left[1 + \frac{b^{2} \sin^{2}{\left(\phi \right)}}{2} + \frac{3 b^{4} \sin^{4}{\left(\phi \right)}}{8} + ... \right]d\phi \\&= 2\pi + \frac{\pi b^2}{2} + \frac{9\pi b^4}{32} + ... \end{aligned}\]
Therefore , \[T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} (1 + \frac{b^2}{4} + \frac{9b^4}{64} + ...) \label{big_angle}\]

Where \(b = \sin{\alpha/2}\)

For small angles, the equation [small_angle] can be achieved as terms of \(\sin{\alpha/2}\) are negligible.

Relation between Amplitude and Period of Oscillation

For small angles, T should remain approximately constant. \[\therefore T \approx 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\]

For quite larger angles, in Equation [big_angle] the powers of b higher than 2 can be neglected. Then, \[T \approx 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} (1 + \frac{b^2}{4} )\] After using small angle approximation, \[T \approx 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} (1 + \frac{\alpha^2}{16} )\] Hence the \(\alpha-T\) graph should be a parabola.

Relation between Length of the pendulum and Period of Oscillation

For a small angle, period of oscillation is given by the equation (5). \[T =2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\] Hence, \[T^2 \propto L\]

Determining which power of time Period has the best linear relationship with the length of the string of the pendulum

Suppose \(T^n \propto L\) for some power n. \[\therefore T^n = kL\] Where k is the proportionality constant. By taking the logarithms of both sides, \[n \ln{T} = \ln{L} + \ln{k}\] \[\ln{T} = \frac{1}{n}\ln{L} + \frac{1}{n}\ln{k}\] If gradient of the \(\ln{T}\) vs \(\ln{L}\) graph is m, m and n are interrelated as, \[n = \frac{1}{m}\] Therefore, n can be determined and n = 2 can be confirmed experimentally.

Determining gravitational acceleration

Using Equation (5), \[T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{g} \cdot L\] Therefore, if the gradient of the \(T^2\) vs \(L\) graph is m, \[m = \frac{4\pi^2}{g}\] Hence, \[g = \frac{4\pi^2}{m}\]

Relation between pendulum’s mass and Period of Oscillation

It is easy to deduce from Equation (5) or Equation (9) that there is no any relationship between pendulum’s mass and its period of oscillation.

Therefore, it can be predicted that the coefficient of determination (\(r^2\)) between pendulum’s mass and the period of oscillation should have a value closer to 0 than \(\pm1\).
If \(T_i\) is the period corresponding to the pendulum mass of \(m_i\) and there were \(n\) number of measurements that have been taken,

\[\begin{aligned} \text{cov}(m,T) & = E(mT) - E(m)E(T) \\ & = \sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{m_iT_i}{n} - \left(\sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{m_i}{n}\right)\left(\sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{T_i}{n}\right) \\ \text{var}(m) & = \sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{m^2_i}{n} - \left(\sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{m_i}{n}\right)^2 \\ \text{var}(T) &= \sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{T^2_i}{n} - \left(\sum^n_{i = 1}\frac{T_i}{n}\right)^2\\ r^2 & = \frac{\text{cov}(m,T)^2}{ \text{var}(m) \text{var}(T)} \\ \end{aligned}\]

Determination of the Damping coefficient of an oscillator

If the damping of the pendulum caused by the friction of air, then the damping fore is directly proportional to the velocity of the pendulum.
Hence, \[F_d = -bv = -bL\frac{d\theta}{dt}\] Where b is the damping coefficient.
Hence equation (1) becomes, \[\begin{aligned} \tau & = -mg\sin{\theta}\cdot L -bL\frac{d\theta}{dt} \\ \therefore \frac{d^2 \theta}{dt^2} & = -\frac{g}{L}\sin{\theta} -\frac{b}{m}\frac{d\theta}{dt} \end{aligned}\] Then the amplitude is given by, \[A(t) = A_0 e^{-\frac{bt}{2m}}\]

The amplitude of the image of the bob in a recorded frame in pixels (\(A_p\)) is proportional to the amplitude of the bob. Hence, \[A_p (t) = A_{p,0}e^{-\frac{bt}{2m}}\]

By taking the natural logarithm of both sides, \[\ln{A_p} = \frac{-b}{2m} t + \ln{A_{p,0}}\]

Hence if the gradient of the \(\ln{A_p}\) vs \(t\) graph is \(\hat{m}\), \[\begin{aligned} \hat{m} & = \frac{-b}{2m} \\ b & = -2m \hat{m} \end{aligned}\]

Procedure

Investigating the relationship between the Amplitude and the time period of oscillations.

The experimental setup was assembled as shown in Figures 1 and 2. Investigating the relationship between the Length of the pendulum and the time period of oscillations.

The experimental apparatus was constructed as shown in Figures 1 and 2 , with the mass of the pendulum bob maintained at a constant value. The bob was displaced by \(10^0 \pm 0.5^0\) for multiple pendulum lengths, and the duration of 30 complete oscillations was recorded for each length.

Investigating the relationship between the mass and the time period of oscillations.

The setup was assembled as shown in the figures. The mass of the bob was measured using an electronic balance. Then, the given piece of clay was broken into six different pieces, and their masses were measured. The length of the pendulum was kept constant (\(67.10\pm 0.05cm\)). By attaching the masses one by one with minimal effect on the center of gravity of the bob and maintaining the initial amplitude at a constant value (\(10^0 \pm 0.5^0\)), the time required for 30 oscillations was measured.

Determination of the damping coefficient.

The setup was assembled as shown in the figures. The length of the pendulum was kept constant. After setting the initial amplitude as \(10^0\), the bob was released while being recorded by the mobile phone camera a rate of 60 fps. The recorded duration was about 300 seconds.

As the recorded video file was sizable, pre-processing was conducted using Blender 4.0.2.0.

The tracker software was used to track the position of the ball, and the resulting values were exported to a CSV file. Then, the amplitudes were detected using the Python libraries pandas , scipy , and numpy .

Discussion

Investigating the relationship between the Amplitude and the time period of oscillations.

The experimental data did not match the expected theoretical curve for \(\theta\) and time. (See graph 3) This discrepancy may have been caused by random errors in the measurements taken by the researcher. The error bars on the graph only represent the propagation of error, and the true errors could be better determined by measuring multiple sets of data and calculating the standard deviation. Additionally, the precision of the dataset could be increased by using tracker software for data analysis, rather than manual measurements.

Investigating the relationship between the Length of the pendulum and the time period of oscillations.

The graph 4 indicates that the value of n is \(2.0 \pm 0.5\), which empirically demonstrates the interrelationship between T and m as \(T^2 \propto m\). By plotting the graph \(T^2\) vs \(m\), an experimental value of \((9.7 \pm 0.7) m s^{-2}\) for the gravitational acceleration, g, was obtained. Comparing this to the true value of \(9.7773 ms^{-2}\) , the experimental value can be accepted. By employing the methods described above, the accuracy of this measurement could be further improved.

Investigating the relationship between the mass and the time period of oscillations.

The graph 6 suggests that the mass (m) and period (T) of the simple pendulum are not strongly interrelated, as the distribution of the data points appears random. If the experiment was conducted with a wider range of masses, the resulting correlation coefficient could be even closer to zero, further indicating the lack of a strong relationship between the two variables.

Determination of the damping coefficient.

According to the graph 7 , an empirical value of \((1.6 \pm 0.3) \times 10^{-4}kgs^{-1}\) could be obtained for the damping constant. But assuming the surrounding air obeys the Stokes’ law the expected value for the damping constant should be about \(3.4\times 10^{-6} kgs^{-1}\).

\[\begin{aligned} F = bv &= 6\pi \eta a v\\ \therefore b &= 6\pi \eta a\\ &= 6\pi \times 1.8 \times 10^{-5} Pas \times 0.01m \end{aligned}\]

This discrepancy may be caused by the friction in the pivot and in the string.

Conclusion

The experiments investigated the relationships between the period of a simple pendulum and its initial amplitude, length, and mass. Although the \(theta\) vs \(T\) graph did not match the predicted curve, the theoretical predictions of the period’s independence on mass and dependence of length (\(T^2 \propto \L\)) were confirmed by the experiments. Additionally, the gravitational acceleration at the experiment location was calculated using the pendulum’s measured period, and the accuracy of this calculation was analyzed. (\(9.7 \pm 0.7 ms^{-2}\)) Finally, in the last experiment, the damping coefficient was determined to be \((1.6\pm0.3)\times 10^{-4} kgs^{-1}\), assuming the damping was caused by frictional forces.
