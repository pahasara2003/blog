Introduction

Prisms and diffraction gratings are commonly used optical instruments in physics. The deviations of light rays through prisms and diffraction gratings are primarily caused by the properties of propagating waves: refraction and diffraction. The purpose of these experiments is to determine the index of refraction of a prism and the diffraction constant of a diffraction grating.

In experiment 01, a spectrometer is used to determine the index of refraction of a prism for sodium light (\(\approx589 \hspace{3pt} nm\)). The experiment aims to establish the refractive index value by calculating the prism angle and the angle of minimum deviation.

Experiment 02 determines the grating constant using the atomic spectrum of mercury. The spectrometer can readily measure the deviation of the spectral lines of mercury caused by the grating. With the known wavelengths of the spectral lines (436 nm, 546 nm, 577 nm and 579 nm ) and the separation between the grating slits, the grating constant can be estimated.

Theory

Experiment 01

Calculating the refractive index of the prism material \((n)\)

Refractive index of a material is defined as the ration of speed of light in vacuum to that in the material. \[n = \frac{v_{material}}{c}\]

According to the Snell’s law, for a given pair of media, the ratio of the sines of angle of incidence \({\displaystyle \theta_{1}}\) and angle of refraction \(\theta_{2}\) is equal to the refractive index of the second medium with regard to the first \(\displaystyle n_{21}\) which is equal to the ratio of the refractive indices \(\displaystyle {\tfrac {n_{2}}{n_{1}}}\) of the two media

Consider a light ray passing through the prism as shown in Figure 1. If the refractive indices of air and prism material are \(n_a\) and \(n\) respectively, \[\frac{n}{n_a} = \frac{\sin{i}}{\sin{r_1}} = \frac{\sin{e}}{\sin{r_2}}\] Since \(n_a\approx1\) this becomes, \[n = \frac{\sin{i}}{\sin{r_1}} = \frac{\sin{e}}{\sin{r_2}}\] Using \(PMN\Delta\) it is easily shown that, \[P\hat{M}N = \frac{\pi}{2} - r_1 \hspace{20pt} \text{and} \hspace{20pt} P\hat{N}M = \frac{\pi}{2} - r_2\] Since, \[A + P\hat{M}N + P\hat{N}M = \pi\] \[A = r_1 + r_2\] using \(OMN \Delta\), \[\begin{aligned} O\hat{M}N + M\hat{N}O + M\hat{O}N &= \pi \\ \therefore (i-r_1) + (e - r_2) + (\pi - D) &= \pi \\ \end{aligned}\] Thus, \[i + e = D + (r_1 + r_2)\\ \] From equation (2), \[r + e = A + D\]

At \(D_{min}\), \(i = e\)
From equation (3), \[i = \frac{A + D_{min}}{2}\] From equation (2), \[r_1 = \frac{A}{2}\] Finally from (2) and (3), \[n = \frac{\sin{(A + D_{min})/2}}{\sin{A/2}}\] Hence, by measuring the angle of minimum deviation \((D_{min})\) and the angle of the prism \((D_{min})\), refractive index (n) can be evaluated.

Measuring the angle of minimum deviation \((D_{min})\)

If the left-hand side and right-hand side scale readings at the straight-through position are \(T_{0L}\) and \(T_{0R}\) and at the minimum deviation, \(T_{L}\) and \(T_{R}\) respectively.

Minimum deviation using the left-hand side scale \(D_L\), \[D_L = |T_L - T_{0L}|\] and minimum deviation using the right-hand side scale \(D_R\), \[D_L = |T_R - T_{0R}|\] Then the angle of minimum deviation, \[D_{min} = \frac{D_L + D_R}{2}\]

Measuring the angle of the prism \((A)\)

By considering Figure (4), if the left-hand side and right-hand side scale readings at \(T_1\) are \(T_{1L}\) and \(T_{1R}\) and at \(T_2\), \(T_{2L}\) and \(T_{2R}\) respectively.

Prism angle using the left-hand side scale \(A_L\), \[A_L = \frac{|A_{1L} - T_{2L}|}{2}\] and prism angle using the right-hand side scale \(A_R\), \[A_R = \frac{|A_{1R} - T_{2R}|}{2}\] Then, by taking the average, the angle of prism, \[A = \frac{A_L + A_R}{2}\]

The \(D-i\) curve

It is easily shown that, \[2i + \theta = 180^0\]\[i = \frac{180^0-\theta}{2}\]

If the left-hand side and right-hand side scale readings at the straight-through position are \(T_{0L}\) and \(T_{0R}\) and at the deviation, \(T_{L}\) and \(T_{R}\) respectively.

Deviation using the left-hand side scale \(D_L\), \[D_L = |T_L - T_{0L}|\] and deviation using the right-hand side scale \(D_R\), \[D_L = |T_R - T_{0R}|\] Then the angle of deviation, \[D = \frac{D_L + D_R}{2}\] The calculated \(i\) and \(D\) values are used to plot the \(D-i\) curve.

Experiment 02

Diffraction grating and grating constant

A diffraction grating consists of evenly spaced (\(d\)) slits. Due to diffraction, these slits act as individual sources of light rays. After constructive and destructive interference of these multiple light rays, the result will be evenly spaced lightening regions.

These lighted lines formed by constructive interference of coherent light rays. To make two light rays coherent, the difference in paths they took (\(\Delta x\)) should be an integer multiple of the wavelength (\(\lambda\)).

Thus \[\Delta x = n \lambda\] As shown in the Figure (7), \[\Delta x = d \sin{\theta}\] Therefore, \[d \sin{\theta} = n \lambda\]

Using the Hg spectrum, a group of such lines can be created. Since their wavelengths are known, by measuring the angles (\(\theta\)), \(d\) can be evaluated.

The diffraction constant(N) is defined as the number of lines (slits) per a millimeter . If d is measured in millimeters, N is given by, \[N = \frac{1}{d}\]

Experiment 01

Adjustment of the Spectrometer for parallel light using Schuster’s Method

Although the simplest way to focus the collimator and telescope for a parallel beam of light is to view a distant object through these components and adjust them until a sharp image is observed, due to environmental constraints, the ’Schuster’s method’ was implemented for this adjustment.

  1. The telescope eyepiece was adjusted until a sharp image of the cross wires was displayed.

  2. The collimator’s slit was made thin and vertical and was illuminated by the sodium lamp.

  3. The prism was placed on the prism table with its center coinciding with the center of the prism table. Then the prism table was rotated until the refracting face AB was directed towards the collimator. Hence the light beam incident on the AB surface was refracted towards the AC surface.



    Afterwards, the image from the AC surface had been observed by naked eye and position \(T_0\) was roughly determined.Subsequently, the telescope was positioned at \(T_o\) and adjusted until the image was centered by cross wires.

  4. Following that, the prism table was turned so that the slit is image shifted towards the directed path of the rays from the collimator and reached at the position of minimum deviation. Next the telescope was also moved to that position and centered the cross wires on the image. (See Figure 10). This position can be easily identified as the image began to turn after achieving the minimum deviation.

  5. Next the telescope was moved away from the directed path so that the telescope was directed for about \(6^\circ\) larger deviation from the minimum deviation point.Then the telescope was clamped.

  6. Then the prism table also moved such that the image was coincided with cross wires again.As there are two possible incident angles that gives the same deviation (Figure (11)), there are two positions for the prism which gives the same result.



    Then only the telescope was adjusted so that a sharp image of the slit was displayed.

  7. After that, the prism table was rotated in the proper direction so that the cross wires coincided with the image at the other position.

    Then only the collimater was adjusted so that a sharp image of the slit was displayed.

  8. The prism table was rotated to the first position and repeated the steps 6 and 7 until a sharp image was gained at the both positions.

Error Analysis

Experiment 01

Uncertainty of the angle of the prism (\(\delta A\))

Since \[A_L = \frac{|T_{2L}-T_{1L}|}{2}\] Using propagation of error, \[\begin{aligned} (\delta A_L)^2 &= \left( \frac{\partial A_L}{\partial T_{2L}} \right)^2 (\delta T_{2L})^2 + \left( \frac{\partial A_L}{\partial T_{1L}} \right)^2 (\delta T_{1L})^2 \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 (0.5')^2 + \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 (0.5')^2 \\ \therefore \delta A_L &= \pm \hspace{3pt} \frac{0.5'}{\sqrt{2}} \end{aligned}\] Uncertainty of the \(A_R\) value is also the same.

Then using, \[A = \frac{A_R + A_L}{2}\] Uncertainty of A, \[\begin{aligned} (\delta A)^2 &= \left( \frac{\partial A}{\partial A_L} \right)^2 (\delta A_L)^2 + \left( \frac{\partial A}{\partial A_R} \right)^2 (\delta A_R)^2 \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \left(\frac{0.5'}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \left(\frac{0.5'}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2 \\ \therefore \delta A &= \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.3' \end{aligned}\]

Uncertainty of the angle of the minimum deviation (\(\delta D_{min}\))

Since \[D_L = |T_{L}-T_{0L}|\] Using propagation of error, \[\begin{aligned} (\delta D_L)^2 &= \left( \frac{\partial D_L}{\partial T_{L}} \right)^2 (\delta T_{L})^2 + \left( \frac{\partial D_L}{\partial T_{0L}} \right)^2 (\delta T_{0L})^2 \\ &= (1)^2 (0.5')^2 + (1)^2 (0.5')^2 \\ \therefore \delta D_L &= \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.5\sqrt{2}' \end{aligned}\] Uncertainty of the \(D_R\) value is also the same.

Then using, \[D_{min} = \frac{D_R + D_L}{2}\] Uncertainty of \(D_{min}\), \[\begin{aligned} (\delta D_{min})^2 &= \left( \frac{\partial D_{min}}{\partial D_L} \right)^2 (\delta D_L)^2 + \left( \frac{\partial D_{min}}{\partial D_R} \right)^2 (\delta D_R)^2 \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \left(0.5'\sqrt{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \left(0.5'\sqrt{2}\right)^2 \\ \therefore \delta D_{min} &= \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.5' \end{aligned}\]

Uncertainty of the refractive index of the prism (\(\delta n\))

\[n = \frac{\sin(\frac{A + D_{min}}{2})}{\sin(\frac{A}{2})}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \therefore \frac{\partial n}{\partial D_{min}} &= \frac{\cos{\frac{A + D_{min}}{2}}}{2\sin{\frac{A}{2}}} \\ &= \frac{\cos{\frac{ 60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5' + 38^0 \hspace{3pt}40.0'}{2}}}{2\sin{\frac{ 60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5'}{2}}}\\ &= 0.651 \end{aligned}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \therefore \frac{\partial n}{\partial A} &= \frac{\sin{\frac{A}{2}}\cos{\frac{A + D_{min}}{2}} -\cos{\frac{A}{2}}\sin(\frac{A + D_{min}}{2}) }{2\sin^2{\frac{A}{2}}} \\ &= \frac{\sin{\frac{60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5'}{2}}\cos{\frac{60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5' + 38^0 \hspace{3pt}40.0'}{2}} -\cos{\frac{60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5'}{2}}\sin(\frac{60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5' + 38^0 \hspace{3pt}40.0'}{2}) }{2\sin^2{\frac{60^0 \hspace{3pt}1.5'}{2}}} \\ &= -0.662 \end{aligned}\]

Using propagation of error, \[\begin{aligned} (\delta n)^2 &= \left( \frac{\partial n}{\partial D_{min}} \right)^2 (\delta D_{min})^2 + \left( \frac{\partial n}{\partial A} \right)^2 (\delta A)^2 \\ &= (0.651)^2 \left(\frac{0.5 \cdot \pi}{60 \cdot 180}\right)^2 + (-0.662)^2 \left(\frac{0.3 \cdot \pi}{60 \cdot 180}\right)^2 \\ \therefore \delta n &= \pm \hspace{3pt} 10^{-4} \end{aligned}\]

Uncertainty of the angles of the deviations (\(\delta D\))

Since \[D_L = |T_{L}-T_{0L}|\] \[D_R = |T_{R}-T_{0R}|\] \[D = \frac{D_R + D_L}{2}\]

Using the calculation for the uncertainty of the angle of the minimum deviation , \[\delta D = \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.5'\]

Experiment 02

Uncertainty of \(\theta_{colour}\)

Since \[\theta_L = |\theta_{1L}-\theta_{SL}|\] \[\theta_R = |\theta_{1R}-\theta_{SR}|\] \[\theta_{colour} = \frac{\theta_R + \theta_L}{2}\]

Comparing with the calculation for the uncertainty of the angle of the minimum deviation , \[\delta \theta_{colour} = \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.5'\]

Uncertainty of \(d\) values for each colour (\(\delta d\))

since \(n=1\), \[\begin{aligned} \lambda &= d \sin(\theta) \\ d &= \frac{\lambda}{\sin(\theta)}\\ \frac{\partial d}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\lambda \cos{\theta}}{\sin^2{\theta}} = \frac{d}{\tan{\theta}} \end{aligned}\] Then, \[\begin{aligned} (\delta d)^2 &= \left( \frac{\partial d}{\partial \theta} \right)^2 (\delta \theta)^2\\ \therefore \delta d &= \pm \hspace{3pt} \frac{d}{\tan{\theta}} \cdot \frac{0.5 \cdot \pi}{60 \cdot 180} \end{aligned}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \text{Violet line} &\longrightarrow \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.9 nm\\ \text{Green line} &\longrightarrow \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.7 nm\\ \text{Yellow 1 line} &\longrightarrow \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.7 nm \\ \text{Yellow 2 line} &\longrightarrow \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.6 nm \end{aligned}\]

Uncertainty of the average of \(d\) values \(\delta \overline{d}\)

Error due to Propagation of error

since \(\overline{d} = \frac{\sum{d}}{4}\), Using error propagation, \[\begin{aligned} (\delta \overline{d})^2 &= \sum_{i=1}^{4} \left(\frac{\partial \overline{d}}{\partial d_i}\right)^2 (\delta d_i)^2 = \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 (0.9^2 + 0.7^2 + 0.7^2 + 0.6^2) \\ \therefore \delta \overline{d} &= \pm \hspace{3pt} 0.4 \hspace{3pt}nm \end{aligned}\]

Standard deviation

\[\begin{aligned} \sigma_{d} &= \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{(d_i - \overline{d})^2}{n(n-1)}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{4} \frac{(d_i - 1657)^2}{12}} \\ &= 2.7\hspace{3pt} nm \approx 3 \hspace{3pt} nm \end{aligned}\]

Therefore the uncertainty of the average of \(d\) values is \(3 \hspace{3pt} nm\)

Uncertainty of Grating Constant (\(\delta N\))

since \(N = \frac{1}{d}\)
, \[\frac{\partial N}{\partial d} = -\frac{1}{d^2} = -N^2\]

Therefore, \[\delta N = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial d}\right)^2 (\delta d)^2} = \pm 603.5^2 mm^{-2} \cdot 3 \times 10^{-6} mm = \pm \hspace{3pt} 1 mm^{-1}\]

Experiment 01

The experiment was conducted using sodium light. Since Na has almost monochromatic spectrum of around 589 nm (Yellow), the created image of the slit can be observed without any dispersion.

It was important to adjust the collimator and the telescope to parallel light rays before performing the experiment as it maximize the accuracy and precision

Angle of the prism (\(A\)) :

Because the prism relied on is standard, a prism angle of around \(60^0\) is expected. Hence the obtained experimental value of \(60^0 \hspace{3pt} 1.5' \pm0.3'\) agrees with the expected result.

Angle of the minimum deviation (\(D_{min}\)) :

For a prism of a prism angle of \(\approx60^0\) and refractive index of about 1.5, the expected value for the minimum deviation is,

From Equation(5), \[r_1 = \frac{A}{2} \approx 30^0\] From Equation (1), \[\begin{aligned} n = 1.5 = \frac{\sin{i}}{\sin{r_1}} &= \frac{\sin{i}}{\sin{30^0}}\\ \therefore i &\approx 49^0 \end{aligned}\] From Equation (4), \[\begin{aligned} i &= \frac{A + D_{min}}{2} \\ \therefore 49^0&=\frac{60^0 + D_{min}}{2} \\ \therefore D_{min} &\approx 38^0 \end{aligned}\] Since the obtained value from the experiment is \(38^0 \hspace{3pt} 40.0' \pm 0.5'\). The obtained value is acceptable.

Index of refraction of the prism material (\(n\)) :

Since the expected refraction index lies around 1.5, the resulted value of \(1.5167 \pm 0.0001\) is also acceptable.

\(D-i\) Curve

Unfortunately, the experimental values of \(D\) and \(i\) did not result the predicted curve. Instead, they had approximately a linear relationship. The result was due to a mistake in the experimental measurement taking procedure.The explanation is as follows:

  • Assume that the deviation for \(\theta=90^0\) has already been measured. When the telescope is moved straight through, the reading is zero (since the platform has not altered).

  • The telescope is now shifted till the right side scale reading reaches \(85^0\) (\(\theta\) measurement). After that, the platform is turned clockwise to center the reflected imagine on the cross wires.

  • The incidence angle for \(\theta=90^0\) is \(45^0\), while for \(\theta=85^0\) it is \(47^0 \hspace{3pt} 30'\). Hence, the platform is turned \(2^0 \hspace{3pt} 30'\) degrees clockwise. Therefore, the new straight through reading would be \(2^0 \hspace{3pt} 30'\) (on right side scale). But there \(0^0 \hspace{3pt} 0'\) was entered as the right side reading. Hence, the deviation is not equal to \(|T-T_0|\), but \(|T-T_0| + 2^0 \hspace{3pt} 30'\).

  • After taking the reading for \(\theta=85^0\), the telescope is returned to the straight through position. Because the reading is \(2^0 \hspace{3pt} 30'\), it is zeroed by rotating the platform counterclockwise. As a result, the incident angle is again \(45^0\).

  • Following the zero correction, the telescope is rotated to \(80^0\), and the reflected image is centered by rotating the platform clockwise. Since the new incident angle is \(50^0 \hspace{3pt} 0'\), the platform should be rotated by \(5^0 \hspace{3pt} 0'\). Hence, the straight through position is now \(5^0 \hspace{3pt} 0'\). Thus, the correct deviation is not \(|T-T_0|\), but \(|T-T_0| + 5^0 \hspace{3pt} 0'\)..

  • Since this affected all of the data except \(\theta=90^0\), the proper value is provided by the following equation:

    \[D_{n,\text{corrected}} = D_{n} + (2^0 \hspace{3pt} 30') \cdot (n-1)\]

    Here \(n=1,2,3,4,5,6\)

  • The corrected values for D are given below.

Conclusion

The first experiment aimed to determine the index of refraction of a prism. This was achieved by measuring the prism’s angle and the angle of minimum deviation. The prism angle was found to be \(60^0 \hspace{3pt} 1.5' \pm 0.3'\). However, the \(D-i\) curve did not yield the expected results due to technical errors. Instead, the angle of minimum deviation was measured to be \(38^0 \hspace{3pt} 40.0' \pm 0.5'\) degrees by the indirect method. Based on these measurements, the computed index of refraction of the prism was calculated to be \(1.5167 \hspace{3pt} \pm 0.0001\).

The second experiment aimed to determine the grating constant of a certain diffraction grating. It was determined based on the atomic spectra of mercury (Hg). The spectrometer was used to measure the grating-induced variations in the primary spectral lines violet (436 nm), green (546 nm), and yellow 1 (577 nm) and yellow 2 (579 nm). The calculated value for the grating was \(604 \pm 1 mm^{-1}\), whereas the actual value was \(600 mm^{-1}\).
